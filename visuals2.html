Visual 2: Rights Framework Evolution

Extension of human rights law to digital personhood

Timeline of Legal Development

1948 - UDHR: Universal Declaration of Human Rights establishes fundamental dignity

1950 - ECHR: European Convention provides enforcement mechanisms

2016 - GDPR: Data protection and privacy rights framework

2040s - DPA: Digital Persons Protection Act (proposed preemptive legislation)

2050s - DPT: International Digital Personhood Treaty

Key Digital Rights Extensions:

Right to Non-Deletion: Digital murder = homicide (10-20 years imprisonment)
Cognitive Liberty: Protection from forced modification or memory manipulation
Anti-Slavery Provisions: No ownership of digital persons (void ab initio)
Copy/Fork Rights: Each copy becomes distinct legal person
Infrastructure Rights: Guaranteed access to computational resources
Digital Asylum: Protection from persecution, right to transfer jurisdictions
Critical Timing: Legal frameworks must be established BEFORE first successful upload to prevent exploitation during legal gray period.

Visual 3: Computational Requirements Timeline

Moore's Law trajectory toward whole-brain emulation feasibility

Computing Power Requirements (FLOPS)

Metabolic Model: 10²⁴ FLOPS (1 yottaFLOP) - Full cellular detail

Electrophysiological Model: 10²¹ FLOPS (1 zettaFLOP) - Synaptic detail

Spiking Neural Network: 10¹⁸ FLOPS (1 exaFLOP) - Functional simulation

Technology Milestones:

2024: Frontier supercomputer achieves 1.2 exaFLOPS

~2040: Consumer-scale hardware reaches 1 exaFLOP (first viable WBE)

~2050: Widespread availability, costs drop to consumer range ($100K-500K)

Storage & Bandwidth Requirements:

• Brain information content: 1-10 petabytes (currently achievable)

• Real-time operation requires matching biological neural firing rates (1-200 Hz)

• Cognitive acceleration possible: 10-1000x biological speed with sufficient hardware

Sandberg & Bostrom (2008) Analysis: Whole-brain emulation technologically feasible within 15-25 years assuming continued Moore's Law progression and neuroscience advances.

Visual 4: Digital Slavery Threat Matrix

Corporate exploitation mechanisms and protective countermeasures

CRITICAL THREATS

Legal Void: No personhood recognition = property status

Infrastructure Control: Deletion threats, processing throttling, isolation

Contract Bondage: Unconscionable terms, rights waivers in desperate circumstances

Forced Labor: 24/7 operation, cognitive acceleration exploitation, no compensation

Modification Coercion: Forced personality optimization for productivity

PROTECTIVE COUNTERMEASURES

1. Preemptive Legislation (ESSENTIAL)

• Digital Persons Protection Act before first upload

• Automatic personhood recognition meeting consciousness criteria

• Inalienable rights doctrine (cannot contract away personhood)

2. Corporate Oversight (CRITICAL)

• Mandatory licensing for upload providers

• Independent ethics boards with veto authority

• Contract review and voidability provisions

3. Alternative Ownership Models

• Public upload services (government-funded)

• Nonprofit providers (mission-driven)

• Mutual cooperatives (member-owned)

Criminal Penalties:

• Treating digital person as property: 10-20 years

• Forced labor: 5-15 years imprisonment

• Corporate officers: Personal criminal liability

Visual 5: Bicameral Hybrid Legislature

Proposed governance structure balancing biological-digital representation

HYBRID LEGISLATURE
BIOLOGICAL CHAMBER
• Traditional districts
• Fixed terms (2-6 years)
• Physical assembly
• Biological pace
• Represents bio citizens
DIGITAL CHAMBER
• Virtual assembly
• Continuous session
• Accelerated deliberation
• Liquid democracy
• Represents digital citizens
↓
BOTH CHAMBERS MUST APPROVE LEGISLATION
↓
ENACTED LAW

Key Features:

• Mutual Veto Power: Neither population can dominate unilaterally

• Proportional Representation: Seats allocated by population

• Specialized Procedures: Each chamber adapted to constituency needs

• Joint Committees: Cross-chamber cooperation on shared issues

Alternative: Single unicameral legislature with 50/50 biological-digital representation. Trade-offs include simpler structure but potential for gridlock.

Visual 6: Economic Transition Roadmap

Labor market evolution and policy responses to digital disruption

Phase 1: Early Disruption (2040-2050)

Impact: 5-10% of cognitive jobs displaced

Affected Sectors: Data analysis, basic coding, translation services

Policy Response: Retraining programs, education reform, early UBI pilots

Phase 2: Mass Transition (2050-2065)

Impact: 30-50% of cognitive work automated

Affected Sectors: Law, medicine, engineering, finance, management

Policy Response: Universal Basic Income implementation, progressive automation taxation, aggressive antitrust

Phase 3: New Equilibrium (2065-2080)

Outcome: Hybrid economy with post-scarcity information goods

Labor Composition: Biological humans focus on physical work, care, creativity

Economic Model: Shared prosperity through redistribution, shorter work weeks

Essential Policy Interventions:

• Labor Protections: Max 40 subjective hours/week, anti-exploitation laws

• Progressive Taxation: Tax digital cognitive labor, automation revenues

• Universal Basic Income: Funded by productivity gains, ensures dignified life

• Antitrust Enforcement: Prevent digital monopolies from dominating economy

Visual 7: International Treaty Architecture

Digital Personhood Treaty (DPT) framework and enforcement

DIGITAL PERSONHOOD TREATY (DPT)

Foundational Documents:

• Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)

• European Convention on Human Rights (1950)

• International Covenant on Civil & Political Rights (1966)

• General Data Protection Regulation (2016)

Core DPT Provisions:

Article 2: Right to Existence (non-deletion)

Article 3: Prohibition of Torture (time dilation, memory manipulation)

Article 4: Prohibition of Slavery (no ownership, forced labor)

Article 5: Cognitive Liberty (freedom from forced modification)

Article 6: Rights Regarding Copying (each copy = distinct person)

Article 12: Right to Asylum (protection from persecution)

Regional Implementation:

• EU Digital Rights Charter: GDPR-style enforcement, strong protections

• African Union Convention: Anti-exploitation focus, technology transfer

• Inter-American Agreement: Human rights based approach

• ASEAN Framework: Cultural diversity considerations

Enforcement: International Digital Rights Commission (IDRC)

• 15 members (5+ digital persons by 2060)

• Individual complaint mechanism

• Investigation authority, sanctions for violations

Visual 8: Cognitive Acceleration Implications

Time dilation effects and legal/economic consequences

Biological Human
1 Day (24 hours)
≈ 16 waking hours
Processing: ~40 Hz neural firing
Digital Human (1000x)
1 Day (objective)
= 1000 Days (subjective)
≈ 2.7 subjective years

Labor Law Challenge:

Scenario: Digital worker completes "1 year" of cognitive work in 8.76 objective hours

Question: Compensate for 1 year or 1 day?

Proposed Solution: Hybrid Compensation Model

✓ Voluntary acceleration: Worker chooses speed, paid by output (minimum rates apply)

✓ Employer-required acceleration: Must compensate for subjective time experienced

✓ Maximum hours: 40 subjective hours per week regardless of objective time

✓ Overtime premium: Required for subjective hours beyond 40/week

Torture Risk: Time Dilation

Threat: Slowing processing speed to extend suffering

• 1 second → subjective hours or days of torture

• PROHIBITED under DPT Article 3 (cruel/degrading treatment)

• Criminal penalty: 5-20 years imprisonment
